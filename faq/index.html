General Lawn Care Questions

Common questions about maintaining a healthy lawn in Palmdale, Lancaster, and the Antelope Valley.

How often should I mow my lawn in the Antelope Valley?

During the growing season (spring and summer), mow weekly to maintain optimal lawn health. In fall and winter, you can reduce to bi-weekly mowing as grass growth slows. Never remove more than one-third of the grass blade height in a single mowing to avoid stressing your lawn.

What is the best grass type for Palmdale and Lancaster?

For the High Desert climate, we recommend warm-season grasses like Bermuda grass or Buffalo grass. These varieties are drought-tolerant, heat-resistant, and thrive in alkaline soil. Tall fescue is also popular for its deep root system and ability to stay green year-round with proper watering.

How much should I water my lawn in the desert?

In summer, water deeply 2-3 times per week in the early morning (before 8 AM). Aim for 1-1.5 inches of water per week total. In winter, reduce to once per week. Deep, infrequent watering encourages deeper root growth and drought resistance. Avoid daily shallow watering, which promotes weak roots.

Why does my lawn have brown patches?

Brown patches in Antelope Valley lawns are commonly caused by: (1) Underwatering or uneven irrigation coverage, (2) Fungal disease from overwatering, (3) Grub damage from beetle larvae, (4) Dog urine spots, or (5) Compacted soil preventing water absorption. Identify the cause first, then treat accordinglyâ€”we can help diagnose and fix the issue.

What height should I cut my grass?

For Bermuda grass, maintain 1-1.5 inches. For tall fescue, keep it at 2.5-3 inches. Taller grass shades the soil, reduces water evaporation, and develops deeper rootsâ€”critical for High Desert conditions. Adjust your mower height seasonally: slightly taller in summer heat, shorter in cooler months.

Should I bag or mulch grass clippings?

Mulching is better for your lawn. Grass clippings decompose quickly and return nitrogen to the soil, reducing fertilizer needs. Only bag clippings if the grass is excessively long, wet, or diseased. Mulching also saves time and reduces yard waste.

Desert Landscaping & Xeriscaping

Expert guidance for water-efficient, beautiful desert landscapes in the Antelope Valley.

What is xeriscaping and is it right for my yard?

Xeriscaping is water-efficient landscaping using drought-tolerant plants, mulch, and efficient irrigation. It's ideal for the Antelope Valley because it reduces water bills by 50-75%, requires less maintenance, and thrives in our climate. You can have a beautiful yard with native plants, decorative rock, and strategic green spaces without the high water costs of traditional lawns.

What are the best plants for High Desert landscaping?

Top choices include: Desert marigold, California poppy, lavender, rosemary, agave, yucca, and palo verde trees. These plants are native or adapted to our alkaline soil, extreme heat, and low rainfall. They require minimal water once established and add color and texture year-round.

How do I deal with alkaline soil in Palmdale?

Antelope Valley soil typically has a pH of 7.5-8.5 (alkaline). To improve it: (1) Add sulfur or gypsum to lower pH, (2) Mix in compost to improve drainage and nutrients, (3) Choose alkaline-tolerant plants, or (4) Use raised beds with imported soil for vegetables and acid-loving plants. Soil amendments work best when applied in fall or early spring.

Can I have a green lawn and save water?

Yes! Reduce your lawn size to high-traffic areas only, and replace the rest with xeriscape. Install a smart irrigation controller that adjusts for weather. Use drought-tolerant grass varieties. Aerate annually to improve water absorption. Even a 50% lawn reduction can cut water use by 30-40% while maintaining curb appeal.

What mulch is best for desert landscaping?

Decomposed granite (DG) and decorative rock are excellent for the High Desert. DG is affordable, compacts well for pathways, and comes in natural colors. Rock mulch (3/4" to 1.5") doesn't decompose, suppresses weeds, and reflects heat. Avoid wood mulch in full sunâ€”it dries out quickly and needs frequent replacement in our climate.

How often should I water desert plants?

Newly planted desert plants need weekly watering for the first 3-6 months to establish roots. Once established, most need water only every 2-4 weeks in summer and monthly (or less) in winter. Overwatering is the #1 killer of desert plantsâ€”they're adapted to drought and prefer dry conditions.

Irrigation & Water Management

Maximize water efficiency and keep your irrigation system running smoothly in the High Desert.

How do I know if my sprinkler system is working efficiently?

Signs of inefficiency include: (1) Dry spots or brown patches despite regular watering, (2) Water pooling or runoff, (3) Sprinkler heads spraying onto sidewalks or driveways, (4) Unusually high water bills, or (5) Visible leaks or broken heads. Run your system during the day once per season to inspect coverage and make adjustments.

Should I use drip irrigation or sprinklers?

Drip irrigation is ideal for desert plants, shrubs, trees, and vegetable gardensâ€”it delivers water directly to roots with 90% efficiency. Sprinklers work better for lawns and large turf areas. Many Antelope Valley homes use both: drip for landscaping beds and sprinklers for grass areas.

When is the best time to water my lawn?

Water between 4 AM and 8 AM. Early morning watering reduces evaporation loss (which can be 50%+ in afternoon heat), prevents fungal diseases from overnight moisture, and ensures water reaches roots before the day's heat. Avoid evening wateringâ€”it keeps grass wet overnight, promoting disease.

How can I reduce my water bill while keeping my yard green?

Key strategies: (1) Install a smart irrigation controller (saves 20-30%), (2) Fix leaks immediately, (3) Adjust sprinkler heads to eliminate overspray, (4) Water deeply but less frequently, (5) Reduce lawn size by 25-50%, (6) Add mulch to retain soil moisture, and (7) Use drought-tolerant plants in non-lawn areas.

What are signs my irrigation system needs repair?

Watch for: Broken or stuck sprinkler heads, uneven spray patterns, water geysers (indicating broken pipes), soggy areas that never dry, controller malfunctions, or zones that won't turn on. In the Antelope Valley, hard water and mineral buildup can also clog drip emittersâ€”flush your system annually.

Do I need to winterize my irrigation system in Palmdale?

Yes, but it's simpler than in colder climates. While we rarely get hard freezes, occasional overnight freezes can damage exposed pipes and valves. Drain above-ground components, insulate backflow preventers, and adjust your controller for reduced winter watering. Most in-ground systems don't need full winterization.

Seasonal Lawn Care Tips

What to do each season to keep your Antelope Valley yard looking its best year-round.

What should I do for my lawn in spring?

Spring prep: (1) Rake and remove winter debris, (2) Aerate compacted soil, (3) Apply pre-emergent weed control in March, (4) Fertilize warm-season grasses as they green up, (5) Repair bare spots with seed or sod, (6) Increase watering frequency as temperatures rise, and (7) Resume weekly mowing when grass starts actively growing.

How do I protect my lawn during Antelope Valley summers?

Summer survival tips: (1) Raise mowing height by 0.5 inches to shade roots, (2) Water deeply 2-3 times per week in early morning, (3) Avoid fertilizing in extreme heat (over 95Â°F), (4) Don't mow during peak heatâ€”wait for cooler mornings or evenings, (5) Watch for heat stress (wilting, blue-gray color) and water immediately if seen.

What fall maintenance does my yard need?

Fall checklist: (1) Aerate and overseed cool-season grasses in September, (2) Apply fall fertilizer to strengthen roots, (3) Reduce watering frequency as temperatures drop, (4) Trim trees and shrubs before winter winds, (5) Clean gutters and remove fallen leaves, (6) Mulch garden beds for winter protection, and (7) Prepare irrigation system for occasional freezes.

Do I need to mow my lawn in winter?

In the Antelope Valley, warm-season grasses go dormant and need little to no mowing in winter. Cool-season grasses (like tall fescue) stay green and may need monthly mowing if actively growing. Reduce mowing frequency to every 2-4 weeks depending on growth. Never mow frozen grassâ€”wait for it to thaw.
