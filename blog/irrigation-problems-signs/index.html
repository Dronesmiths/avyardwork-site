5 Signs Your Irrigation System is Wasting Water (And Money)

By Brian, AV Yard Work  |  January 17, 2026  |  4 min read

In the Antelope Valley, where water is expensive and conservation is critical, a malfunctioning irrigation system can waste hundreds of dollars per month. Here are five telltale signs your system needs attentionâ€”and what to do about them.

Sign #1: Soggy Spots That Never Dry

What it looks like: Persistent wet areas in your lawn, even days after watering. Grass may be yellowing or dying in these spots.

What's causing it: Underground pipe leak, broken sprinkler head, or valve that won't shut off completely.

Why it matters: A single leak can waste 6,000+ gallons per monthâ€”adding $50-100 to your water bill.

How to fix it:

  • Turn off your system and mark soggy areas
  • Dig carefully to locate the leak
  • Replace damaged pipes or fittings
  • For deep leaks or valve issues, call a professional

Sign #2: Brown Patches Despite Regular Watering

What it looks like: Dead or dying grass in specific areas, while surrounding lawn is healthy.

What's causing it: Clogged sprinkler heads, misaligned spray patterns, or zones not turning on.

Why it matters: You're paying to water your lawn, but some areas aren't getting any water at all.

How to fix it:

  • Run your system during daylight to inspect coverage
  • Clean clogged heads with a small wire or toothpick
  • Adjust spray patterns to eliminate gaps
  • Replace broken heads ($3-15 each)
  • Test each zone at the controller to ensure it activates

Sign #3: Unusually High Water Bills

What it looks like: Water bill suddenly 30-50%+ higher than normal, with no change in usage habits.

What's causing it: Hidden leaks, controller malfunction causing overwatering, or stuck valves running continuously.

Why it matters: In Palmdale/Lancaster, summer water rates can exceed $5 per 100 cubic feet. A major leak costs $100-300/month.

How to fix it:

  • Check your water meter before and after a 2-hour period with no water useâ€”if it moved, you have a leak
  • Inspect visible pipes, valves, and backflow preventers
  • Run each zone and time how long it runsâ€”compare to controller settings
  • Look for water pooling near valve boxes

Sign #4: Water Geysers or Visible Leaks

What it looks like: Water shooting up from the ground, pooling around sprinkler heads, or running down the street.

What's causing it: Broken main line, cracked fitting, or sprinkler head knocked off by mower/foot traffic.

Why it matters: This is an emergencyâ€”you're losing gallons per minute. Turn off your system immediately.

How to fix it:

  • Shut off irrigation at the main valve or controller
  • For broken heads: unscrew and replace (simple DIY)
  • For pipe breaks: call a professionalâ€”requires excavation and proper repair

Sign #5: Uneven Spray Patterns or Overspray

What it looks like: Sprinklers watering sidewalks, driveways, or neighbor's property. Some areas get double coverage while others get none.

What's causing it: Misaligned heads, wrong nozzle types, or system designed poorly.

Why it matters: You're wasting 20-40% of water on non-lawn areas. Plus, wet sidewalks promote algae growth and can damage foundations.

How to fix it:

  • Adjust spray direction by rotating head tops
  • Replace full-circle heads with half-circle or quarter-circle nozzles where appropriate
  • Install matched precipitation rate (MPR) nozzles for even coverage
  • Add or relocate heads to eliminate gaps and overlaps

Bonus: Hard Water & Mineral Buildup

The Antelope Valley has notoriously hard water. Over time, mineral deposits clog drip emitters and sprinkler nozzles, reducing efficiency.

Solution: Flush your system annually and soak clogged parts in vinegar to dissolve calcium buildup.

When to Call a Professional

Some irrigation problems are easy DIY fixes, but others require expertise:

  • Call a pro for: Underground pipe leaks, valve replacements, controller malfunctions, system redesigns
  • DIY-friendly: Replacing sprinkler heads, adjusting spray patterns, cleaning clogged nozzles
